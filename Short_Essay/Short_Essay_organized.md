# 1. What is the meaning of culturalism and the process of Sinification?

**Logic:** Define Culturalism → Process of Sinification (mechanisms + examples) → Conclusion & Synthesis

---

## Paragraph 1: Defining Culturalism

### Must-have Points

**Culturalism** was a **political community model** in imperial China based on **shared cultural values** and **Confucian doctrines**, not ethnicity or race. (Source: HUMA2635_5.pdf p.3)

Through **education and imitation** (教化与模仿), "barbarians" could become part of this community, distinguishing themselves from those who did not share these values. (Source: HUMA2635_5.pdf p.3)

The **Huaxia** group categorized surrounding non-Huaxia peoples as the **four barbarians**: **eastern Yi** (东夷), **western Rong** (西戎), **northern Di** (北狄), **southern Man** (南蛮). (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf p.3)

Culturalism contrasts with an **ethnic-based identity** founded on a **self-description of a people as Han**. (Source: HUMA2635_5.pdf p.3)

> **Bonus:** Culturalism was premised on the belief that China was the **cultural center of the world** ("All under Heaven" 天下). Foreign rulers were expected to recognize Chinese superiority through the **ke-tou** (叩头) ritual. (Source: HUMA2635_3.pdf p.4)
>
> Culturalism saw people as **subjects of an absolute authority**, while modern nationalism regards them as **citizens with equal rights**; the shift involves a transformation from **soft boundaries** to **hard boundaries**. (Source: HUMA2635_3.pdf p.3)

---

## Paragraph 2: The Process of Sinification

### Must-have Points

**Sinification** (漢化) is the **assimilation** (同化) of non-Han people into Han identity through **military conquest** (军事占领) and **cultural influence** (文化影响); expansion **southward** was generally easier than **northward** because northern areas were populated by **nomadic tribes** on inhospitable terrain. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf pp.11,13)

**Non-Han dynasties** that ruled over Han Chinese include: **Northern Wei** (Topa 拓跋), **Liao** (Khitan 契丹), **Jin** (Jurchen 女真), **Yuan** (Mongol 蒙古), and **Qing** (Manchu 满清). (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf p.13)

**Institutional mechanisms**:
- The **Tusi system** (土司制度) since the **Yuan dynasty**: native chieftains were absorbed into the government hierarchy, and ethnic groups were classified as **"cooked"** (shu 熟, ripe) if incorporated, or **"raw"** (sheng 生) if outside. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf p.23)
- **Gaitu guiliu** (改土歸流): under **Ming and Qing**, native offices were replaced by **regular administration** where sinification had progressed. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf p.24)

**Concrete example**: The **Nanzhao kingdom** (南诏) requested **Tang imperial education** for its princes, captured Chengdu artisans and scholars, and **10 of the 22 Dali kings** became **Buddhist monks**. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf pp.14,21)

> **Bonus:** The process was **not one-way**: both "we" (Han/min) and "they" (yi/man) evolved; the standards of sinification — **Chinese script, household registration, civil examinations, ancestral sacrifice** — themselves changed over time. This is **David Faure's cultural legitimacy theory**. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf p.12)
>
> **State nationalism** after 1911 demanded **"nation-building"** that rejected the idea that Chinese history and culture were purely a Han affair. (Source: HUMA2635_3.pdf p.7)

---

## Paragraph 3: Conclusion & Synthesis

### Must-have Points

**Culturalism** provided the **ideological framework** of a culture-based hierarchy; **Sinification** was the **dynamic political and institutional process** that expanded this order.

The practice of **demarcation** (划分界定) between Han and non-Han continued from Ming-Qing to the present; its mechanisms have been **remarkably consistent**. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf p.30)

**"Majorities are made, not born"** : ethnic identities are constructed through **marking differences**, and the idea of a majority presupposes **homogeneity**. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf p.29)

> **Bonus:** The early 20th-century affirmation of **"five major nationalities"** and the PRC's later classification of **55 minorities** are essentially **continuations of imperial demarcation exercises**. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf p.31)

# 2. What is Gaitu Guiliu policy in Chinese history?

**Logic:** Define Gaitu Guiliu → Preceding Tusi system → Implementation & Significance

---

## Paragraph 1: Defining Gaitu Guiliu

### Must-have Points

**Gaitu guiliu** (改土歸流) refers to the policy of **replacing native chieftains with regular government officials** appointed by the imperial state. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf p.24)

The term literally means **"replacing native with regular"** — abolishing the **hereditary tusi** (世袭土司) system and integrating minority territories into the **standard provincial administration**.

This policy was implemented primarily during the **Ming and Qing dynasties**, aiming to **consolidate state control** over frontier regions.

> **Bonus:** The policy was **not applied uniformly**; it was enacted **selectively** in areas where the sinification of minority peoples had progressed sufficiently to make native officials unnecessary. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf p.24)
>
> Gaitu guiliu reflects the long-term imperial strategy of **administrative absorption** rather than purely military conquest.

---

## Paragraph 2: The Tusi System as Predecessor

### Must-have Points

Since the **Yuan dynasty**, the imperial government had set up the **Tusi system** (土司制度), **absorbing native non-Han leaders** into the lower levels of the government hierarchy. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf p.23)

Native chieftains received official titles prefixed with the character **Tu** (土), meaning "native," and these positions were **hereditary** (世袭).

Ethnic groups brought under the Tusi system were classified as **"cooked"** (shu 熟, ripe), while those remaining outside were termed **"raw"** (sheng 生). (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf p.23)

The **Han Chinese were encouraged to migrate** into these areas, accelerating **cultural integration** and preparing the ground for administrative reform. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf p.23)

> **Bonus:** In some regions, efforts were made to **weaken individual native officials** by **increasing their numbers**, thereby reducing the size of the territory each controlled. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf p.24)
>
> This **fragmentation strategy** was a **precursor** (前奏/预演) to later Gaitu guiliu, making it easier for the state to eventually dismantle native power structures.

---

## Paragraph 3: Implementation and Significance

### Must-have Points

Based on the **progress of sinification**, the Ming and Qing governments initiated the change from native to regular administration, formally called **Gaitu guiliu**. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf p.24)

The policy served to **centralize imperial authority**, **eliminate hereditary power bases** that could challenge the state, and bring frontier populations into the **standard tax system**.

Gaitu guiliu was a crucial mechanism in the **territorial and administrative integration** of what is now **southwestern China** into the Chinese state.

> **Bonus:** The policy can be understood within the broader framework of the **culturalism-to-nationalism transformation**: imperial absorption of frontiers later became the territorial basis for **modern nation-state claims**. (Source: HUMA2635_3.pdf pp.3-4)
>
> Along with the Tusi system, Gaitu guiliu illustrates how the imperial state's approach to minorities was **pluralistic** (多元化) but ultimately **centralizing**, aiming at **control** rather than deep cultural assimilation in the short term. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf p.26)

# 3. What are the main arguments of the debates about New Qing History and cultural legitimacy theory?

**Logic:** Sinicization Thesis → New Qing History Counter Arguments → Cultural Legitimacy Theory as Resolution

---

## Paragraph 1: The Sinicization Thesis (Traditional View)

### Must-have Points

According to the **Sinicization thesis**, Chinese history is interpreted as a narrative of **continuing dynastic replacement** from Han to Tang, Song, Yuan, and Qing. **Non-Han groups** who invaded the central plain gained governance legitimacy **only after being educated by Confucianism** and becoming **culturally Han**. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf p.2)

This view holds that the **Han Chinese were advanced** not because of territorial scale but because of their **cultural power to assimilate** other groups. This process of **civilizing — Sinicization** — is presented as the **central thread** of Chinese history, including during the Qing dynasty. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf p.2)

> **Bonus:** The Sinicization narrative historically operated on a geographic axis centered on the **Yellow River plain and the Yangtze River delta**, treating **Inner Asia** as remote frontiers. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf p.4)
>
> This framework was classically articulated by **Ping-ti Ho** (1968), who argued the **Manchus were effectively Sinicized** into the Chinese cultural world. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf p.2)

---

## Paragraph 2: New Qing History's Counter Arguments

### Must-have Points

**New Qing historians** **separate the concept of the Qing empire from China**. They argue the Manchu success lay in using **different governance models** for different peoples in different regions, not in Sinicization. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf p.3)

The **Manchu rulers** maintained their distinct ethnic identity through the **Eight Banner system** (八旗制度), the **Manchu language**, and traditions of **hunting and riding**. The Qing court also adopted **Tibetan Buddhism and Shamanism** for its rituals. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf p.3)

The **Qing emperors performed multiple roles simultaneously**: **Confucian emperors** for the Han, **Khans** for the Mongols, and **Reincarnated Buddhas** for the Tibetans. Thus, **Inner Asia** (Mongolia, Tibet, Xinjiang) were **different components of the Qing empire**, not merely peripheral frontiers of "China." (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf p.4)

> **Bonus:** New Qing historians (James Millward) proposed the model of **"Five Nations Under Heaven"** (天下五族), in which **Han, Manchu, Tibetan, Mongol, and Muslim** occupy **parallel positions** in relation to the Qing imperial house. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf p.11)
>
> This argument has **political implications**: if the Qing was an empire distinct from "China," then **Xinjiang, Tibet, and Mongolia** were not inherently part of a Chinese territorial inheritance. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf p.14)
>
> The Qing summer capital at **Chengde** (Bishu Shanzhuang 避暑山庄) served as a new political center replicating key cultural monuments of China, Tibet, and Central Asia, symbolizing the empire's **multi-ethnic integration**. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf pp.9-10)

---

## Paragraph 3: Cultural Legitimacy Theory as Resolution

### Must-have Points

**Cultural legitimacy theory** was proposed as a response to this debate. It argues that even if "Han" may be taken as an ethnic term, **"min"** (民) was a **legal status**. The process of sinification was **not one-way**: the standards of being "Chinese" — **Chinese script** (汉字), **household registration** (户籍), **civil examinations** (科举), **ancestral sacrifice** (祭祖) — were themselves **evolving** among both "we" and "they" from the 15th to the 20th centuries. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf p.12)

**Local society** did not accept imperial imposition **without negotiation**. The Chinese imperial state was built on a **loose structure** that presumed much greater political unity than its institutions could actually deliver. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf p.15)

Whether or not local rituals were standardized, they could be **legitimized** through real or imagined **master-disciple transmission** (师徒传承), through the **power of writing** and **state authority**. This long process produced **overlapping but not identical bodies of legitimizing symbols** across different regions. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf p.12)

> **Bonus:** The study of **religious markers** may be a way to explain Chinese political and cultural unification through the perspective of a **legitimizing process** over varied geographic regions. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf p.15)
>
> This theory bridges the debate by showing how **both center and periphery** (边缘) participated in negotiating cultural identity, rather than one simply absorbing the other. **The history of China and the history of the parts are equally important as the history of the whole.** (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf p.15)

# 4. What are "the three metaphors" and their relationship with the civilizing centers?

**Logic:** Civilizing projects concept → Metaphor 1 (women) → Metaphor 2 (children) → Metaphor 3 (ancient) → Synthesis

---

## Paragraph 1: Introducing Civilizing Projects and the Three Metaphors

### Must-have Points

Over the last few centuries, **"minorities" have been subjected** to a series of attempts by dominant powers to transform them, to make them more like the transformers, or to **"civilize"** them. (Source: HUMA2635_11.pdf p.2)

This is a kind of interaction in which the **civilizing center** interacts with peripheral peoples in terms of a particular kind of **inequality**. (Source: HUMA2635_11.pdf p.2)

Three major **civilizing centers** have operated in Chinese history: the **Confucians** (儒家), the **Western missionaries** (传教士), and the **Communists** (共产主义). (Source: HUMA2635_11.pdf p.4)

The civilizers portray peripheral peoples through **three metaphors**: as **women**, as **children**, and as **ancient/historical** (先祖/历史). (Source: HUMA2635_11.pdf pp.9,18,20)

> **Bonus:** Each civilizing center began with a different assumption of hierarchy: the Confucians based on **moral values**, the missionaries on the **revealed truth of Christianity**, and the Communists on a **scientific scale of material stages** of social process. (Source: HUMA2635_11.pdf p.4)

---

## Paragraph 2: First Metaphor — Ethnic Minorities as Women

### Must-have Points

The civilizers have seen peripheral peoples (minorities) as both **erotic and promiscuous** (情色与放荡) in their behavior, as being at a **lower level of culture** where they have not yet learned proper civilized morals of **sexual repression and/or hypocrisy** (性压抑/虚伪). (Source: HUMA2635_11.pdf p.9)

An **objectification** (物化) of the category of civilizers and peripheral peoples occurs: the **Han majority and non-Han minorities** is similar to the relationship between **male and female**. (Source: HUMA2635_11.pdf p.13)

Both minorities and women are **subordinate categories** (从属类别) under the **tutelage of the state's civilizing project**. (Source: HUMA2635_11.pdf p.13)

The logic is circular: **minorities are like women, so women represent minorities**. (Source: HUMA2635_11.pdf p.13)

> **Bonus:** This metaphor positions the Han (or European) center as implicitly **male**, which by contrast legitimately assumes the task of civilizing and the **superior political and moral position** from which the project can be carried out. (Source: HUMA2635_11.pdf p.25)

---

## Paragraph 3: Second Metaphor — Ethnic Minorities as Children

### Must-have Points

Seeing peripheral peoples as **children** represents them as both **inferior and educable** (低等但可教育). (Source: HUMA2635_11.pdf p.18)

The task of the civilizing center is to **civilize them**, because children can be taught and raised to maturity. (Source: HUMA2635_11.pdf p.18)

In official discourse, the Han and minorities are described as **xiongdi minzu** (兄弟民族), or **big-brother / little-brother minzu** (老大哥/小兄弟), with the Han occupying the **elder sibling role**. (Source: HUMA2635_11.pdf p.18)

> **Bonus:** The child metaphor reinforces the **temporal dimension** (时间维度): just as children grow into adults, peripheral peoples can, in theory, develop into full participants in the civilizing center's modernity. This makes the inequality appear **temporary and benevolent** (暂时、仁慈) rather than permanent and oppressive.

---

## Paragraph 4: Third Metaphor — Ethnic Minorities as Ancient, and Synthesis

### Must-have Points

Peripheral peoples are portrayed as **ancient, unchanged**, not so far along on the same scale as the people of the civilizer center. (Source: HUMA2635_11.pdf p.20)

If the **ancestors of the center** were once as primitive as the periphery is now, then the project of trying to civilize them has **some chance of success**. (Source: HUMA2635_11.pdf p.20)

Almost any difference between center and periphery can be translated into a **historical gap** (历史差距) between the way things are done now and the way they were done at the **evolutionary stage** represented by the peripheral peoples. (Source: HUMA2635_11.pdf p.22)

> **⭐ 关键总结 (Synthesis):** By portraying peripheral peoples as **female, childlike, and historical**, the Han (or European) center — which is by implicit contrast **male, adult, and modern** — legitimately assumes the task of **civilizing**, and with it the **superior political and moral position** from which the civilizing project can be carried out. (Source: HUMA2635_11.pdf p.25)

> **Bonus (Example):** The **Mosuo** (摩梭) are treated as a **"historical remainder"** — their matrilineal system places them at an **earlier evolutionary stage** according to historical materialism. (Source: HUMA2635_11.pdf p.21; connection to HUMA2635_6.pdf p.8)
>
> After **1999**, **tourism and mass media** have added new dimensions beyond the three metaphors, with more **imaginary elements and performances** of local culture being represented for tourist consumption. (Source: HUMA2635_11.pdf pp.31-32)

# 5. Compare two minority autonomous regions: Xinjiang and Tibet

**Logic:** Geography → Cultural Differences → Relationship with Han Majority

---

## Paragraph 1: Geography

### Must-have Points

**Xinjiang**'s geography is characterized by **oases separated by great distances**, which fostered **social and cultural diversity**. (Source: HUMA2635_8.pdf p.37)

**Tibet** is defined by **high altitude and marginal lands**, where indigenous peoples relied on **cold-resistant crops** and **inland tea**. (Source: HUMA2635_10.pdf p.8)

---

## Paragraph 2: Cultural Differences

### Must-have Points

**Xinjiang** — Identity is tied to **Islam**, being one of the **10 Muslim nationalities** in China. **Kashgar** is the **conservative Islamic heartland**; **Turpan** is a **less conservative historic center** of Uyghur culture. Unlike the **"scattered" distribution** (散居) of the Hui, Xinjiang's Muslims are more **concentrated in oases**. (Source: HUMA2635_7.pdf p.3; HUMA2635_8.pdf pp.37, 39)

**Tibet** — Society centers on **Lamaist Buddhism** (喇嘛教), where the **Dalai Lama** holds **temporal and spiritual rule** as a **Living Buddha** (活佛). (Source: HUMA2635_10.pdf p.6)

> **Bonus:** While groups like the **Hui** used **traditional Chinese ideologies** to interpret Islamic traditions, Xinjiang's **Uyghur culture** remains more distinct, centered around oases like Kashgar. (Source: HUMA2635_7.pdf p.56; HUMA2635_8.pdf p.52)
>
> The avoidance of **pork** serves as a **vital social forbidden** for identity formation among Xinjiang's Muslim groups. (Source: HUMA2635_7.pdf p.56)
>
> The Qing government reformed Tibetan administration by requiring the Dalai Lama to be reincarnated in a **commoner's family** and supervised by a resident **amban** (驻藏大臣). (Source: HUMA2635_10.pdf p.7)

---

## Paragraph 3: Relationship with the Han Majority

### Must-have Points

**Xinjiang** — The relationship is defined by **massive Han immigration**: the Han population rose from **10% (1949)** to approximately **40%** recently. (Source: HUMA2635_8.pdf p.9)

**Tibet** — The Han population remains **small (around 8%)** , and the relationship was historically maintained through the **tea and horse trade** (茶馬貿易). (Source: HUMA2635_10.pdf pp.8, 19)

> **Bonus:** Unlike the **Hui**, who expanded through **migration and intermarriage with Han** (often adopting Han children), Xinjiang and Tibet exhibit **less demographic blending** and more **distinct ethnic boundaries**. (Source: HUMA2635_7.pdf p.55)
>
> Historically, Tibetans and the Han interacted economically through the **tea and horse trade**, a barter system exchanging interior agricultural goods like tea for nomad horses. (Source: HUMA2635_10.pdf p.19)

# 6. Describe social changes and Buddhism development in Tibet

**Logic:** Traditional Theocratic Society → Social Changes (1911–1950s) → Tibetan Buddhism under Changing Regimes

---

## Paragraph 1: Traditional Theocratic Society and Centrality of Buddhism

### Must-have Points

In Tibet, the **temporal as well as the spiritual rule** of the **Dalai Lama** was recognized; the system of **reincarnation** was set up in **1447 during the Ming dynasty**. (Source: HUMA2635_10.pdf p.6)

This **Lamaist hierarchy** was also supported by the **Qing government**, applying to both Mongolia and Tibet. The **Court of Colonial Affairs** (理藩院) handled relations. (Source: HUMA2635_10.pdf p.6)

The Dalai Lama is the reincarnation of **Avalokitesvara** (观世音菩萨); the **Fifth Dalai Lama** proclaimed the **Panchen Lama** the reincarnation of **Amitabha** (阿弥陀佛), the teacher of Avalokitesvara. Although Amitabha is higher in the Buddhist view, the Panchen Lama's **temporal powers were minimal**, while the Dalai Lama's were **supreme**. (Source: HUMA2635_10.pdf p.9)

> **Bonus:** Tibetan Buddhism was **not a monolithic entity**: the **Kangba** (康巴) people spoke different dialects and had customs distinct from Lhasa, and indigenous populations were **fiercely independent**. (Source: HUMA2635_10.pdf p.8)
>
> **Tea and butter** were staples linked to Buddhist culture; tea compressed into **bricks** came from inland China, and **buttered tea** was believed to warm the body, fight hunger, and aid digestion — a cultural tie between Tibet and the rest of China since the **7th century**. (Source: HUMA2635_10.pdf p.16)

---

## Paragraph 2: Social Changes from 1911 to the 1950s and Beyond

### Must-have Points

The **1911 revolution** enabled Tibetans to **free themselves of Qing troops**. In **1914**, Britain called the **Simla Conference** (西姆拉会议) proposing that an **amban** with only 300 soldiers reside in Lhasa and no other Chinese troops be sent, but the republican government **refused to acknowledge** the convention. (Source: HUMA2635_5.pdf p.48)

The **Kuomintang government** created the province of **Xikang** (西康) in **1939**, ruled by **Liu Wenhui** (刘文辉), extending its boundary far west of the **Jinsha River**. In **1940**, the **14th Dalai Lama** was inaugurated, while the Nationalist government continued to treat the **Panchen Lama with great care**. (Source: HUMA2635_5.pdf p.48)

After **1949**, a system of **regional autonomy** was provided for minority-concentrated areas. In **1950**, the **PLA crossed the Jinsha River** and marched to **Changdu** (昌都). In **1951**, the **Seventeen Point Agreement** (十七条协议) was signed: the PLA would enter Tibet to consolidate national defense, and the Tibetan army would be integrated into the PLA. (Source: HUMA2635_6.pdf p.19)

**Democratic reforms** were gradually introduced; party structures were created and **People's governments** replaced traditional structures. (Source: HUMA2635_6.pdf p.27)

> **Bonus:** The **Yan'an period's minorities policy** served as a working model for future enlargement; the **Inner Mongolia Autonomous Region (1947)** provided a precedent for Tibet's later autonomous status. (Source: HUMA2635_6.pdf p.18)
>
> Social transformation was justified by **historical materialism**: Tibet's traditional **feudal-theocratic system** was placed at a particular stage that needed to progress through democratic reform toward socialism. (Source: HUMA2635_6.pdf pp.7-8, 27)

---

## Paragraph 3: Developments in Tibetan Buddhism under Changing Regimes

### Must-have Points

**Reincarnation politics** were often manipulated by powerful families. **Qing reforms** required the Dalai Lama to be reincarnated in a **commoner's family**, and the resident **amban** (驻藏大臣) supervised these regulations, leading to a **bureaucratization of Tibetan administration** and an increase in the Dalai Lama's power vis-à-vis noble families. (Source: HUMA2635_10.pdf p.7)

The **13th Dalai Lama** used an **alliance with Britain** to strengthen his government's control over semi-feudal components, including areas of the **9th Panchen Lama**. This prompted the Panchen Lama to turn to the **Chinese central government** for aid against their common enemies — the Dalai Lama and Britain. (Source: HUMA2635_10.pdf p.9)

After **1951**, the CCP's policy toward religion in minority areas emphasized **freedom of religion** and respect for customs, while also incorporating **"patriotic upper strata"** into governmental hierarchy and promoting the party line against both **Great Han chauvinism** and **local nationalism**. (Source: HUMA2635_6.pdf pp.16,22)

> **Bonus:** The CCP's early strategy included **"doing good and making friends"** : small PLA work teams won trust by dispensing seeds, tools, and learning local languages, while propagandizing party doctrine and nationalities policy — a **soft approach** that gradually transformed the religious-political landscape. (Source: HUMA2635_6.pdf p.24)
