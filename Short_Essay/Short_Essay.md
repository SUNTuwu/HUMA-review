## 分段：
## Paragraph 1: Defining Culturalism
### Must-have Points
Culturalism was a political community model in imperial China based on shared cultural values and Confucian doctrines, not ethnicity or race. (Source: HUMA2635_5.pdf, p.3)
Through education and imitation, "barbarians" could become part of this community, distinguishing themselves from those who did not share these values. (Source: HUMA2635_5.pdf, p.3)
The Huaxia group categorized surrounding non-Huaxia peoples as the four barbarians: eastern Yi, western Rong, northern Di, southern Man (東夷, 西戎, 北狄, 南蛮). (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf, p.3)
Culturalism contrasts with an ethnic-based identity founded on a self-description of a people as Han. (Source: HUMA2635_5.pdf, p.3)
### Bonus Points
Culturalism was premised on the belief that China was the cultural center of the world ( "All under Heaven" 天下). Foreign rulers were expected to recognize Chinese superiority through the ke-tou ritual. (Source: HUMA2635_3.pdf, p.4)
Culturalism saw people as subjects of an absolute authority, while modern nationalism regards them as citizens with equal rights; the shift involves a transformation from soft boundaries to hard boundaries. (Source: HUMA2635_3.pdf, p.3)
## Paragraph 2: The Process of Sinification
### Must-have Points
Sinification (漢化) is the assimilation (同化) of non-Han people into Han identity through military conquest and cultural influence; expansion southward was generally easier than northward because northern areas were populated by nomadic tribes on inhospitable terrain. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf, pp.11,13)
Non-Han dynasties that ruled over Han Chinese include the Northern Wei (Topa), Liao (Khitan), Jin (Jurchen), Yuan (Mongol), and Qing (Manchu). (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf, p.13)
Institutional mechanisms:
The Tusi system (土司制度) since the Yuan dynasty: native chieftains were absorbed into the government hierarchy, and ethnic groups were classified as "cooked" (shu, ripe熟) if incorporated, or "raw" (sheng生) if outside. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf, p.23)
Gaitu guiliu (改土歸流): under Ming and Qing, native offices were replaced by regular administration where sinification had progressed. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf, p.24)
Concrete example: the Nanzhao kingdom requested Tang imperial education for its princes, captured Chengdu artisans and scholars, and 10 of the 22 Dali kings became Buddhist monks. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf, pp.14,21)
### Bonus Points
The process was not one-way: both "we" (Han/min) and "they" (yi/man) evolved; the standards of sinification—Chinese script, household registration, civil examinations, ancestral sacrifice—themselves changed over time. This is David Faure's cultural legitimacy theory. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf, p.12)
State nationalism after 1911 demanded "nation-building" that rejected the idea that Chinese history and culture were purely a Han affair. (Source: HUMA2635_3.pdf, p.7)
## Paragraph 3: Conclusion & Synthesis
Culturalism provided the ideological framework of a culture-based hierarchy; Sinification was the dynamic political and institutional process that expanded this order. (Synthesis of above)
The practice of demarcation between Han and non-Han continued from Ming-Qing to the present; its mechanisms have been remarkably consistent. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf, p.30)
"Majorities are made, not born": ethnic identities are constructed through marking differences, and the idea of a majority presupposes homogeneity. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf, p.29)
Bonus: The early 20th-century affirmation of "five major nationalities" and the PRC's later classification of 55 minorities are essentially continuations of imperial demarcation exercises. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf, p.31)

EXAMPLE:
Culturalism, as presented in the course, refers to the dominant political community model in imperial China. It was based not on race or ethnicity but on shared cultural values and Confucian doctrines. According to the lecture slides, through education and imitation, even "barbarians" could become members of this community, thereby distinguishing themselves from others who did not share those values (HUMA2635_5, p.3). The Huaxia people classified surrounding non-Huaxia groups as the four barbarians: eastern Yi, western Rong, northern Di, and southern Man (HUMA2635_2, p.3). This culture-based identity contrasted sharply with an exclusive ethnic identity defined by Han self-description.
Sinification (漢化) is the historical process through which non-Han peoples were assimilated into this cultural and political order. It proceeded both by military conquest and cultural influence, with southward expansion generally being easier than northward expansion due to the nomadic, inhospitable terrain of the north (HUMA2635_2, pp.11,13). Several non-Han dynasties, such as the Yuan and Qing, ruled over Han populations. Institutionally, the Tusi (native chieftain) system, established since the Yuan dynasty, incorporated local leaders into the imperial hierarchy and categorized groups as "cooked" (ripe) if integrated, or "raw" if they remained outside (HUMA2635_2, p.23). Where sinification had advanced sufficiently, the Ming and Qing governments implemented Gaitu guiliu, replacing native chieftains with regular bureaucratic administration (HUMA2635_2, p.24). The Nanzhao and Dali kingdoms illustrate cultural sinification: Nanzhao princes studied in Tang Chang’an, and captured artisans were brought to the capital, while ten of the twenty-two Dali kings abdicated to become Buddhist monks.

The slides note that identities of both "Han" and "non-Han" evolved through negotiation, as the very criteria of sinification changed over time (HUMA2635_4, p.12). Furthermore, "majorities areminoritiesot born” the demarcation between Han and minority has been a continuous act of boundary-making from imperial times to the present (HUMA2635_2, p.29). Thus, culturalism and sinification together shaped the dynamic, multi-layered process of identity formation that underlies China's long-term cultural and political integration.


# What is Gaitu Guiliu policy in Chinese history?
> Logic:
* Define Gaitu guiliu and state its historical purpose (replacement of native administration; regular bureaucratic官僚化 governance)
* Explain the preceding system (Tusi) and why reform was needed (Tusi system; native chieftains; hereditary 世袭 titles; "cooked" vs "raw"; encouraged immigration; progressive 渐进 sinification)
* Describe the implementation and consequences of Gaitu guiliu (Ming and Qing dynasties; weakening native power; increasing numbers; integration into imperial system)

## 分段：
## Paragraph 1: Defining Gaitu Guiliu
### Must-have Points
Gaitu guiliu (改土歸流) refers to the policy of replacing native chieftains with regular government officials appointed by the imperial state. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf, p.24)
The term literally means "replacing native with regular" — abolishing the hereditary tusi (世袭土司) system and integrating minority territories into the standard provincial administration.
This policy was implemented primarily during the Ming and Qing dynasties, aiming to consolidate state control over frontier regions.
### Bonus Points
The policy was not applied uniformly; it was enacted selectively in areas where the sinification of minority peoples had progressed sufficiently to make native officials unnecessary. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf, p.24)
Gaitu guiliu reflects the long-term imperial strategy of administrative absorption rather than purely military conquest. (Synthesis idea)

## Paragraph 2: The Tusi System as Predecessor
### Must-have Points
Since the Yuan dynasty, the imperial government had set up the Tusi system (土司制度) , absorbing native non-Han leaders into the lower levels of the government hierarchy. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf, p.23)
Native chieftains received official titles prefixed (前缀) with the character Tu (土), meaning "native," and these positions were hereditary.
Ethnic groups brought under the Tusi system were classified as "cooked" (shu, ripe), while those remaining outside were termed "raw" (sheng). (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf, p.23)
The Han Chinese were encouraged to migrate into these areas, accelerating cultural integration and preparing the ground for administrative reform. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf, p.23)
### Bonus Points
In some regions, efforts were made to weaken individual native officials by increasing their numbers, thereby reducing the size of the territory each controlled. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf, p.24)
This fragmentation strategy was a precursor前奏/预演 to later Gaitu guiliu, making it easier for the state to eventually dismantle native power structures.

## Paragraph 3: Implementation and Significance
### Must-have Points
Based on the progress of sinification, the Ming and Qing governments initiated the change from native to regular administration, formally called Gaitu guiliu. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf, p.24)
The policy served to centralize imperial authority, eliminate hereditary power bases that could challenge the state, and bring frontier populations into the standard tax system.
Gaitu guiliu was a crucial mechanism in the territorial and administrative integration of what is now southwestern China into the Chinese state.
### Bonus Points
The policy can be understood within the broader framework of the culturalism-to-nationalism transformation: imperial absorption of frontiers later became the territorial basis for modern nation-state claims. (Source: HUMA2635_3.pdf, pp.3-4; synthesis idea)
Along with the Tusi system, Gaitu guiliu illustrates how the imperial state's approach to minorities was pluralistic多元化 but ultimately centralizing, aiming at control rather than deep cultural assimilation in the short term. (Source: HUMA2635_2.pdf, p.26)

# What are the main arguments of the debates about New Qing History and cultural legitimacy theory?
> Logic:
* Introducing the Sinicization (Sinification) thesis and its core claim (dynastic replacement; cultural assimilation; Confucian education; Han cultural center)
* Present New Qing History's counter arguments (Manchu ethnic identity (八旗制度…); different governance models not Sinification (Qing vs. Other dynasties); Inner Asia not only China frontier; Five Nations Under Heaven天下五族 ‘汉满蒙回藏’)
* Explain cultural legitimacy theory as a resolution (multiply layered state penetration渗透; local agency and negotiation; ritual markers; cultural legitimacy)
## 分段：
## Paragraph 1: The Sinicization Thesis (Traditional View)
### Must-have Points
According to the Sinicization thesis, Chinese history is interpreted as a narrative of continuing dynastic replacement from Han to Tang, Song, Yuan, and Qing. Non-Han groups who invaded the central plain gained governance legitimacy only after being educated by Confucianism and becoming culturally Han. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf, p.2)
This view holds that the Han Chinese were advanced not because of territorial scale but because of their cultural power to assimilate other groups. This process of civilizing — Sinicization — is presented as the central thread线索 of Chinese history, including during the Qing dynasty. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf, p.2)
### Bonus Points
The Sinicization narrative historically operated on a geographic axis centered on the Yellow River plain and the Yangtze River delta, treating Inner Asia as remote frontiers. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf, p.4)
This framework was classically articulated by Ping-ti Ho (1968), who argued the Manchus were effectively Sinicized into the Chinese cultural world. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf, p.2)

## Paragraph 2: New Qing History's Counter Arguments
### Must-have Points
New Qing historians separate the concept of the Qing empire from China. They argue the Manchu success lay in using different governance models for different peoples in different regions, not in Sinicization. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf, p.3)
The Manchu rulers maintained their distinct ethnic identity through the Eight Banner system, the Manchu language, and traditions of hunting and riding. The Qing court also adopted Tibetan Buddhism and Shamanism for its rituals. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf, p.3)
The Qing emperors performed multiple roles simultaneously: Confucian emperors for the Han, Khans for the Mongols, and Reincarnated Buddhas for the Tibetans. Thus, Inner Asia (Mongolia, Tibet, Xinjiang) were different components of the Qing empire, not merely peripheral frontiers of "China." (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf, p.4)
### Bonus Points
New Qing historians(James Millward) proposed the model of "Five Nations Under Heaven" (天下五族), in which Han, Manchu, Tibetan, Mongol, and Muslim occupy parallel positions in relation to the Qing imperial house. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf, p.11)
This argument has political implications: if the Qing was an empire distinct from "China," then Xinjiang, Tibet, and Mongolia were not inherently part of a Chinese territorial inheritance. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf, p.14)
The Qing summer capital at Chengde (Bishu Shanzhuang) served as a new political center replicating key cultural monuments of China, Tibet, and Central Asia, symbolizing the empire's multi-ethnic integration. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf, pp.9-10)

## Paragraph 3: Cultural Legitimacy Theory as Resolution
### Must-have Points
Cultural legitimacy theory was proposed as a response to this debate. It argues that even if "Han" may be taken as an ethnic term, "min" (民) was a legal status. The process of sinification was not one-way: the standards of being "Chinese" — Chinese script汉字, household registration户籍, civil examinations科举, ancestral sacrifice祭祖 — were themselves evolving among both "we" and "they" from the 15th to the 20th centuries. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf, p.12)
Local society did not accept imperial imposition without negotiation. The Chinese imperial state was built on a loose structure that presumed much greater political unity than its institutions could actually deliver. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf, p.15)
Whether or not local rituals were standardized, they could be legitimized through real or imagined master-disciple transmission 师徒传承, through the power of writing and state authority. This long process produced overlapping but not identical bodies of legitimizing symbols across different regions. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf, p.12)
### Bonus Points
The study of religious markers may be a way to explain Chinese political and cultural unification through the perspective of a legitimizing process over varied geographic regions. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf, p.15)
This theory bridges the debate by showing how both center and periphery边缘 participated in negotiating cultural identity, rather than one simply absorbing the other. The history of China and the history of the parts are equally important as the history of the whole. (Source: HUMA2635_4.pdf, p.15)

EXAMPLE:
The debate over late imperial Chinese history centers on two competing frameworks: Sinicization and New Qing History. The Sinicization thesis interprets Chinese history as a continuous narrative of dynastic replacement, arguing that non-Han conquerors gained legitimacy by being educated in Confucianism and assimilating into Han culture (HUMA2635_4, p.2). From this perspective, the Qing dynasty's success stemmed from its adoption of Chinese cultural and political norms.
New Qing historians fundamentally challenge this view. Scholars such as Evelyn Rawski, Mark Elliott, and James Millward separate the concept of the Qing empire from "China" proper. They argue that Manchu rulers deliberately maintained their ethnic identity through the Eight Banner system, Manchu language, and distinct religious practices including Tibetan Buddhism and Shamanism (HUMA2635_4, p.3). Crucially, the Qing emperors performed multiple roles: Confucian monarchs for Han subjects, Khans for Mongols, and Reincarnated Buddhas for Tibetans. According to Millward's "Five Nations Under Heaven" model, Han, Manchu, Tibetan, Mongol, and Muslim peoples occupied parallel positions under Qing imperial rule, with the Chengde summer capital symbolizing this multi-ethnic integration (HUMA2635_4, pp.9-11). Thus, Inner Asia was not a peripheral frontier but a distinct component of a larger empire. This argument carries modern political implications: if the Qing was an empire distinct from China, territories like Xinjiang, Tibet, and Mongolia were not necessarily Chinese by inheritance (HUMA2635_4, p.14).
As a potential resolution, David Faure offers the theory of cultural legitimacy. He argues that "min" was a legal status, and the markers of being "Chinese" — written script, household registration, civil examinations, ancestral sacrifice — were themselves evolving among both Han and non-Han populations from the fifteenth to the twentieth centuries (HUMA2635_4, p.12). The imperial state presumed greater unity than its institutions could actually deliver, and local societies did not accept imposition without negotiation. Instead, cultural symbols were legitimized through master-disciple transmission, the authority of writing, and state recognition, producing overlapping but locally varied forms of identity across China's vast territories (HUMA2635_4, pp.12,15). This theory thus reframes the debate: rather than asking whether the Qing were "Sinicized" or "Manchu," it examines how both center and periphery continuously negotiated cultural identity within a shared but flexible symbolic framework.

According to the course materials, over the last few centuries China's ethnic minorities have been subjected to a series of civilizing projects by dominant powers seeking to transform them. Three major civilizing centers — the Confucians, Western missionaries, and Communists — have operated with different assumptions of hierarchy based respectively on moral values, revealed religious truth, and a scientific scale of material stages (HUMA2635_11, pp.2,4). In representing peripheral peoples to themselves and others, these civilizing centers have relied on three recurring metaphors: minorities as women, as children, and as ancient peoples.
The first metaphor portrays ethnic minorities as women. The civilizers have seen peripheral peoples as erotic and promiscuous, at a lower level of culture where they have not yet learned the "proper civilized morals of sexual repression and/or hypocrisy" (HUMA2635_11, p.9). This objectification creates an analogy whereby the Han majority and non-Han minorities are positioned like male and female — both minorities and women are subordinate categories under the tutelage of the state's civilizing project (HUMA2635_11, p.13).

Seeing peripheral peoples as childlike presents them as both inferior and educable, making the task of the center one of benevolent tutelage (HUMA2635_11, p.18). If the ancestors of the center were once as primitive as the periphery is now, then the project of civilizing them has some chance of success (HUMA2635_11, p.20). Almost any cultural difference can be translated into a historical gap representing different evolutionary stages (HUMA2635_11, p.22).
Together, these three metaphors function as a legitimizing framework. By portraying peripheral peoples as female, childlike, and historical, the Han center — which is by implicit contrast male, adult, and modern — legitimately assumes the task of civilizing and with it a superior political and moral position (HUMA2635_11, p.25). This framework transforms what is fundamentally a relationship of political and economic inequality into one that appears natural, benevolent, and provisional. The assumption of cultural superiority thus becomes an integral aspect of hegemonic rule over China's peripheral peoples.

Despite the lack of large-scale settlement, Tibet maintained strong historical ties with the Han majority through the tea and horse trade, an institutionalized barter economy between inland agriculturists and Tibetan nomads (HUMA2635_10.pdf, p. 19).

EXAMPLE:
Tibet's traditional society was a theocracy in which the Dalai Lama held both temporal and spiritual authority. The reincarnation system, established in 1447 during the Ming dynasty, was supported by the Qing court through the Court of Colonial Affairs and supervised by the resident amban (HUMA2635_10, p.6). The Dalai Lama was revered as the reincarnation of Avalokitesvara, while the Panchen Lama was proclaimed the reincarnation of Amitabha. Although Amitabha ranked higher spiritually, the Panchen Lama's temporal powers were minimal compared to the Dalai Lama's (HUMA2635_10, p.9). This dual religious leadership coexisted with a semi-feudal social structure dominated by noble families and monasteries.
Major social changes began in the early twentieth century. The 1911 revolution allowed Tibetans to expel Qing troops, and the 1914 Simla Conference attempted to redefine Tibet's status, though republican China refused to ratify the agreement (HUMA2635_5, p.48). The Kuomintang established Xikang Province in 1939, and the 14th Dalai Lama was inaugurated in 1940. The decisive transformation came after 1949: in 1951, the Seventeen Point Agreement brought Tibet under the new socialist state, with the PLA entering to consolidate national defense and the Tibetan army integrated into the PLA (HUMA2635_6, p.19). Democratic reforms dismantled the old feudal-theocratic order, party structures replaced traditional authorities, and a regional autonomy framework was gradually implemented.
Buddhism itself evolved amid these political shifts. Qing reforms had already required the Dalai Lama to be reincarnated in a commoner’s family, bureaucratizing Tibetan administration and strengthening the Dalai Lama’s position against the nobility (HUMA2635_10, p.7). The 13th Dalai Lama forged an alliance with Britain, prompting the 9th Panchen Lama to seek Chinese central government support, illustrating how religious authority became entangled with geopolitical rivalries (HUMA2635_10, p.9). Under the People’s Republic, the party line guaranteed freedom of religion and respect for customs, while “patriotic upper strata” were co-opted and both Han chauvinism and local nationalism were officially opposed (HUMA2635_6, pp.16,22). Thus, Tibetan Buddhism moved from being the ideological foundation of a theocracy to a religion operating within a socialist state that promised autonomy but demanded political loyalty — a tension that continues to define Tibet’s social and spiritual life.
