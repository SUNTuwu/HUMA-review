Native chieftain system (Tusi system, 土司制度)
• Since the Yuan dynasty, the native chieftain system has 
been set up.  
• Leaders of native non-Han people were absorbed into the 
government hierarchy on its lower levels; they were given 
ordinary official titles preceding by the character Tu, 
meaning “native”.  Local chieftains received a hierarchy title 
based heredity. 
• The Han Chinese were encouraged to migrate to the 
“barbarian” areas, thus incorporating these areas into the 
regular Chinese administrative system.
• Ethnic groups brought under this system was referred to as 
“cooked” or “ripe” (shu), while those who remained outside 
were termed “raw” (sheng). 

24
Gaitu guiliu 改土歸流
• In some areas progressive sinification of the 
minority peoples has rendered native officials 
unnecessary.  
• Based on the progress of sinification, the Ming 
and Qing instituted a change from native to 
regular government called the Gaitu guiliu.  
• In other areas, efforts were made to weaken the 
power of individual native officials by increasing 
their numbers and thus decreasing the size of 
the area presided over by each.

The Qing Empire 

26
• The goal of dynasties and their policies toward ethnic 
minorities was a pluralistic form of integration that aimed 
at little more than control.  
• Abstention from aggression and a vague commitment of 
loyalty to the emperor and the Confucian values the 
system embodied were sufficient to attain the level of 
integration.  
• The imperial bureaucracy extended its influence no 
further down than the county level.  
• The Han cultural centralism advocates that, ethnic 
minorities should come to them, learn of them, and share 
their privileges, and not the other way around.

The demarcation “Han” and “non-Han” are 
actually fluid
• China’s historical expansion to the south is a way 
of assimilation, but it is also a story of demarcation 
and differentiation.
• The binaries between Chinese and non-Chinese, 
or Han and non-Han are self-evident in the stories 
of assimilation, which tend to focus on the roles of 
Han settlers in the transformation of the native 
population.  
• This transformation has been repackaged and 
reinterpreted as the core of Han ethos.

“Majorities are made, not born.”
• Ethnic identities are rooted in certain “givens” 
and should not be attributed solely to 
considerations for power, status, or material 
gains. 
• The idea of majority is based on a presumption 
of homogeneity, the notion of minority is 
founded on the desire to mark differences.  
• The people they represent are clearly 
interdependent.

• The practice and process of demarcation 
have continued through the Ming to Qing 
period to the present.  
• Although the impetus behind acts of 
demarcation has changed over time, their 
mechanism and functions have been 
remarkably consistent.

Summary 
The affirmation of the presence of five 
major nationalists (min zu民族) in China in 
the nationalist discourse of the early 20th 
century as well as the official classification 
of 55 minority min zu by the People’s 
Republic are both considered essentially a 
continuation of the exercise of 
demarcation in imperial times.
