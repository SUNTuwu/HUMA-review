The Hui as Chinese Islam: 
Qing Zhen 清真
• The earliest Muslim communities were 
descended from the Arab, Persian 波斯人, 
Central Asian, Mongolian Muslim 
merchants, militia, and officials。
• They settled along China’s southeast 
coast and in the northwest in large and 
small numbers from the seventh to the 
fourteenth centuries.

20
Meanings of Qing Zhen
• Pure and True Faith (Qing Zhen Jiao 清真
教), or the Correct Religion, arose in China 
from Sui and Tang dynasties. 
• The statement of the recognition of the 
Lord do not lost yet.  More over, they 
obtained the most Holy Mohammed’s very 
detailed account of the plain commands of 
the True Lord. 

21
• Before the Yuan dynasty, Qing Zhen referred loosely to 
both Islam and Judaism, but, by the Ming dynasty, its 
meaning was generally restricted to the religion of the 
Hui people.
• Qing Zhen Jiao 清真教(The Pure and True Religion) was 
the name officially given to Islam in a fourteenth century 
edict.  
• Ciyuan(《辞源》): “Pure and True lacks desire, it is 
everything that cannot change.” 清真寡欲, 萬物不能易也.
• Liu Sanjie 劉三傑: Islam comprises 3 elements: “The 
purity and truth of Allah, religion, and humankind.” 主的
清真,教的清真和人的清真.

22
• Ma Fuchu馬復初: Qing Zhen in terms of Confucian 
ideals when: “to deny oneself is pure, to restore propriety 
is true.”  
• Ma Fuchu explicitly tied the Islamic concept of Qing 
Zhen to traditional Confucian principles expressed in the 
phrase “denying oneself and restoring propriety.” 克己复
禮.
• Hui scholars in today suggest that Qing Zhen means 
clean and authentic (清洁正式), emphasizing both the 
sanitary and authoritarian aspects of the term.  
• The two aspects of Qing Zhen, purity and truth, define 
important tensions in their identity: Islamic moral purity 
and the authenticity of ethnic ancestry, lifestyle, and 
heritage.

The Hui identity
• The Hui have always been ritually and morally suspect, 
not merely because of their foreign origins, but because 
of their avoidance of pork.
• In China, pork has been the basic meat protein for 
centuries concerns.
• Social tabu in terms of symbolic import and influence on 
social relations.
• Mary Douglas: social pollution is structured in the context 
of marginality and boundedness: people make moral 
distinctions between what they regard as pure and 
impure in the context of social exclusively and inclusivity.  

24
• Social tabu not only concerns with ideological 
purity, but also marks the boundaries of Hui’s 
identity, separating Self from Other.  
• Making the Hui as the pure community,  this 
notion and its wide variety of expression and 
reinterpretation among various Hui communities, 
is basic to their identity.  
 
• The Nationalist state has traditionally labeled 
their faith simply as the “religion of the Hui” (hui 
jiao回教).

History
• As early as the seventh century in Tang dynasty, 
historians documented the presence of large groups of 
foreign merchants dwelling in the southeast coastal 
communities of Canton 廣州, Xiamen 厦門, and 
Quanzhou 泉州.  
• There were increasingly large numbers of mainly Persian 
and Arab Muslims who were grouped with the other 
foreigners as fan ke 番客(barbarian guests).  
• As they settled and took on local spouses, their offspring 
became known as tu sheng fan ke 土生番客.

Under Khubilai Khan’s leadership, the Mongols 
made an official hierarchical distinction between 
four kinds of peoples in China:  
 1. The Mongols 蒙古: the highest level of society; 
 2. The Semu 色目: included other Central Asians, 
Europeans, and Muslims, known as the Hui hui; 
 3. The Han people 漢人: not only Northern 
Chinese, but also Koreans, Khitans, and Jurchen; 
 4. The Nan 南人: the Chinese population in the 
south.

• The term Huihui 回回, or Hui Jiao 回教,

• It received its first official institutionalization

The Hui communities
• The traditional Hui residence are small communities 
clustered around a central mosque, known as Gedimu 
(from Arabic “old”) 格底目／老教, a branch of Sunnite 逊尼.
• Each village centered upon a single mosque is headed by 
an ahong 阿訇(from the Persian, akhun), who was invited 
to teach on a more or less temporary basis.  
• These ahong generally moved every three years from one 
mosque to another on an average. 
   
• A council of senior local elders and ahong were 
responsible for the affairs of each village and the inviting of 
the itinerant imam.
  

34
• The isolation of these individual Gedimu 
communities reveals the importance of 
trade and migration history among the Hui.  
• The Hui can be traced to the descendants 
of migrants from the southeast along the 
Spice Route, and from the northwest along 
the Silk Road, and the Burma Road in the 
south, or the main transport nodes of the 
Yellow River in the north.

Who is the Shaykh? 
•
Sufis believe that Divine Blessing (Ar. baraka) flows from 
God to the charismatic leader (Ar. shaykh, Ch. shihe or 
shehai “篩海”), the head of the Sufi order or brotherhood 
(Ar. tariqa, pl. turuq).  
•
This Baraka (口唤) can be passed to the next generation 
either by biological descent (usually to a son or a brother) or 
by purely spiritual descent (to a favorite disciple).  
•
The legitimacy of that transmission must be ensured in order 
to continue; questioning it can lead to conflict, even violence 
and schism, within the order.  The resulting genealogies, 
whether in Arabic or Chinese, do not resemble Chinese Jiapu 
at all. 
42

• Some Gansu Sufi orders mandated biological descent, 
thus earning the name menhuan (門宦), which means 
the “saintly lineages.”   
• Others required teacher-disciple transmission, 
especially the Qadiriyya, with its celibate clergy living 
in the mosque-hospice (Ar. zawiya, Ch. Daotang 道统) 
complexes.   
• Both forms existed elsewhere in the Muslim world, and 
both had advocates as “the correct way” to transmit 
God’s baraka to a new shaykh.

Social changes after 1911 
• As a result of political events and the influence of foreign 
Muslim ideas, numerous new Hui organization emerged in 
the modernist reforms. 
• The Chinese Muslim Mutual Progress Association (Bejing, 
1912), the Chinese Muslim Educational Association 
(Shanghai, 1925), and the Chinese Muslim Association 
(1925).
• Islam in China has expanded through migration, 
intermarriage, and adoption.  
• Hui frequently adopted Han children in the past, raising 
them as Muslims, and eventually accepting them as Hui.
