China: the land and its peoples
• The areas of Chinese culture, settlement
and political domination, have been
extended greatly over 40 centuries from
their original core in the middle valleys of
the Yellow river and the Yangzi river.
• The “China” that we think of today has
been a long term construction.

• The most fundamental distinction of the land and its
peoples may be called Inner and Frontiers/Outer China:
内地與邊疆。
• The boundary between them is defined in terms of the
contrast between a settled, and frequently irrigated,
intensive farming, and a pastoral economy, sometimes
combined with marginal dry-farming, or with
complementary agricultural enclaves (飛地) like the oases
of the northwestern deserts.
• The two basic regions are of roughly comparable area,
but hardly more than 5% of population live in
Fontier/Outer China. It includes the world’s highest
mountain (Zhumulangma, or Mount Everest, 8848M) and
contains some of the world’s fiercest desert in the Gobi
(戈壁) and the Takla Makan （塔克拉瑪干）, and endless
grass-covered steppes.

•

The population of Inner China has long been
overwhelmingly Han, the term commonly used for ethnic
Chinese.

• The political union of China was only achieved in the 18th
century by Qing dynasty. After the unification the
bureaucratic system of government used for two
millennia in Inner China was not extended to frontier
regions, which was ruled under a variety of jurisdictions.
• Almost all of historic frontiers is divided into so-called
“Autonomous Regions （自治區）” after 1949.
• Not all the Qing domain has been inherited by the
People’s Republic. Outer Mongolia achieved virtual
independence in 1912.

• China has historically had different religions.
The traditional faiths of Inner China were
Confucianism, Daoism, Mahayana
Buddhism, and amalgam of local cults.
• In frontiers, people gave their allegiance to
Lamaist Buddhism, Islam or remnants of
older indigenous systems of beliefs.

• These historical, geographic and physical
features set the conditions of cultural
diversity in China.

• The Qingling 秦嶺 and the Huaihe river 淮河
line divides Inner China into North and South,
at latitude of about 35 degrees north.
• North China has historically been a land of
dry-field farming, mostly wheat and millet.
South China has been the home of wet-field
rice farming. Intensive coastal shipping was
also developed early up and down the
southeastern littoral, making use of the
convenient seasonal alternation of the
monsoon winds.

more local variations, making sweeping cultural
contrasts is too hazardous.
• Based on the above conditions, there remains a
persistent uncertainty as to answer to the
questions: “Who is a Chinese?” and “what is
Chinese culture?”

What is an ethnic nationality
(minzu 民族) in China?
• There are 55 ethnic groups, which the Chinese state recognizes as
its “minority nationalities”.
• The Chinese state follows a definition of nationality laid down by
Stalin in 1913:
• A historically constituted, stable community of people, formed on the
basis of a common language, territory, economic life, and
psychological make-up manifested in a common culture.

• The Hakkas in South China, although culturally, linguistically and
socially quite distinct in the late imperial period of Chinese history, or
the Mosuo living in the Lugu Lake, the state does not recognize
either of these people as a “nationality”.
• It considers the Hakkas as part of the majority Han, and the Mosuo
as a branch of the Naxi.

China’s ethnic minorities
• China is a country with 55 state-recognized minority nationalities, plus
a majority nationally called the Han.
• They constitute a bare 8.89% of total population (2020 census, 125
million. Total population in China is 1411 million);
• Most minority groups live on the land frontiers of China.
• In many cases, the borders as presently demarcated divide a minority
group into two or more states.
• For example: the Shan (Dai) in Thailand and Myanmar as well as in
China, and Mongols in Russia, China, and the Mongolian People’s
Republic.
• The 8.4% of the population occupies 60% of the land area of China.

Ethnic minorities and the state
integration
• Since the 1950s, ethnic groups haven been shifting their
loyalties, expectations and political activities toward a
center, whose institutions assume jurisdiction over, and
responsibility for groups.
• Minority groups retain their respective cultures and other
distinguishing characteristics, presumably with the full
approval of the majority group.
• Minority groups accept certain characteristics from the
dominant group, such as language or style of clothing,
while still maintaining many elements of their traditional
cultures and varying degrees of ethnic identification.
